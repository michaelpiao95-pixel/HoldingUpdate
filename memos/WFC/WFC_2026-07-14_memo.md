# Wells Fargo & Company (WFC) — Earnings Memo
Period: Q2 FY2026 | Report Date: 2026-07-14 | Prepared: 2026-07-14

## 1. Snapshot
- Revenue: $22,622M (+9% YoY from $20,822M; +5% QoQ from $21,446M) — source: [Q2 2026 press release, July 14 2026]
- NII: $12,317M (+5% YoY; +2% QoQ) | Noninterest income: $10,305M (+13% YoY; +10% QoQ)
- EPS: GAAP $2.00 diluted (+25% YoY from $1.60; +25% QoQ from $1.60) — includes $0.04/share discrete tax benefit
- Net income: $6,407M (+17% YoY; +22% QoQ)
- ROTCE: 17.7% | ROE: 15.0% | Efficiency ratio: 60%
- One-line takeaway: Broad beat on revenue and earnings; EPS +25% YoY on NII growth, fee momentum, improving credit, and tight expense control — Q3 dividend raised 11% to $0.50/share

## 2. What happened this quarter

Revenue grew 9% YoY to $22.6B, with both NII (+5%) and noninterest income (+13%) contributing. NII improvement was driven by lower deposit costs, higher loan and investment securities balances, and balance sheet growth in the Markets business, partially offset by the impact of lower rates on floating-rate assets. The $0.04/share discrete tax benefit from resolution of prior-period matters adds $132M to net income; adjusting for this, underlying EPS would be approximately $1.96.

Noninterest income beat sharply at $10,305M (+13% YoY, +10% QoQ), driven by strong venture capital investment performance, higher investment advisory fees on elevated market valuations, higher investment banking fees, and broad fee growth across most other categories. One headwind: the prior-year quarter included a $253M gain on the merchant services joint venture acquisition, which is not repeated. Also, lower lease income reflects the Q1 2026 sale of the rail car leasing business.

Expense discipline was notable — noninterest expense of $13,661M rose only 2% YoY despite the revenue growth, driven by higher revenue-related incentive compensation, technology spending, and advertising, partially offset by lower lease expense and efficiency initiatives (headcount down 7% YoY). The efficiency ratio improved dramatically from 67% in Q1 2026 and 64% in Q2 2025 to 60% this quarter, the best result in years, reflecting the payoff of the asset-cap-era investments.

Credit quality continued to improve. Net charge-offs of $876M (annualized 0.34% of avg loans) were down from 0.45% in Q1 2026 and 0.44% in Q2 2025. Nonperforming assets declined to 0.77% of loans (from 0.86% in Q1 2026). The provision of $914M was down 9% YoY and 19% QoQ, with allowance modestly higher for card and auto (on higher balances) and lower for commercial real estate.

## 3. Guidance & management commentary

WFC does not provide formal quarterly EPS guidance, but CEO Charlie Scharf's commentary was notably confident. Key themes:

- **Broad-based growth**: All four operating segments posted strong revenue growth. Consumer Banking +6%, Wealth & Investment Management +13%, Commercial Banking +6%, Corporate & Investment Banking +16%.
- **Balance sheet capacity unlocked**: Scharf directly referenced the end of the WFC competitive disadvantage: "After years of not being on a level playing field with our competitors because we couldn't grow our balance sheet, we are carefully deploying capital to grow and support our clients." Average loans +12% YoY to $1,026.5B; average deposits +10% to $1,465.6B.
- **Capital return acceleration**: ~$7B in share repurchases in H1 2026; Q3 dividend raised 11% to $0.50/share (pending board approval). SCB remains at minimum 2.5% floor; 2026 Fed stress test did not tighten capital requirements.
- **Economic outlook**: Cautiously optimistic — "Consumers and businesses remain very strong" (lower charge-offs, higher spending, growing savings/investments) but Scharf flagged that "such favorable conditions do not go on forever" and the bank is being selective about growth. No major macro concerns signaled.

Implicit for Q3 2026: Revenue growth trajectory to continue; expense efficiency improvements to persist; credit benign; capital returns (buybacks + higher dividend) ongoing.

## 5. Thesis check

**Thesis: Strongly supported.** This is the quarter the WFC re-rating narrative becomes tangible. With the asset cap still technically in place but balance sheet growing, ROTCE reached 17.7% — approaching the peer group (JPM ~20%, BAC ~15%) — and the efficiency ratio hit 60%, a structural improvement. The bank is now deploying capital at scale, returning ~$7B to shareholders in six months, and all four segments contributed to growth.

Prior risk: persistent NII headwind from repricing and lower rates. Status: NII is still growing (+5% YoY) as the deposit cost relief and loan volume are more than offsetting rate headwinds — thesis holding.

**New risks this quarter**: CET1 at 10.3% (vs. 11.1% a year ago) reflects deliberate growth, not capital erosion, but leaves less buffer if the macro environment turns. The $0.04 tax benefit ($132M) in EPS is not repeatable. Equities +64% YoY in Markets is outstanding but volatile.

Valuation context: At ~$80-85/share [market price not in this release], P/E on $2.00 Q2 EPS annualized (~$7.50-8.00 LTM range) would be ~10-11x — cheap relative to peers at 12-15x. ROTCE at 17.7% should support a price/TCE of ~2.0x [EST].

## 6. Watch items for next quarter

1. **NII trajectory**: With Fed rates stable/cut and deposit betas stabilizing, watch whether NII can sustain $12.0-12.5B/quarter or if rate mix continues to compress net interest margin (2.43% this quarter, declining from 2.47% in Q1 and 2.68% a year ago).
2. **Asset cap removal**: Any Fed announcement on lifting the consent order. This remains the single biggest rerating catalyst — removal would allow WFC to match growth with balance sheet expansion without constraint.
3. **Credit normalization**: Net charge-off rate hit a low of 0.34% this quarter; watch for any re-acceleration in consumer credit card or auto, particularly given rising loan balances.
